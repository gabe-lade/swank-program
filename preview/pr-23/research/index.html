Research

Research by Swank Program members examines issues related to land use, environmental quality, and policy design. Below are recent peer-reviewed publications and working papers conducted by program members.

Recent Publications

Quantifying Threshold Manipulation in the Presence of Rounding: The Case of Lead Monitoring in US Drinking Water

Authors: Tihitina Andarge, Dalia Ghanem, David A. Keiser, and Gabriel E. Lade Year: 2025 Publication: American Economic Review: Insights Link:

Summary: When regulations set specific cutoff points there’s a risk that people might manipulate test results to just barely pass the threshold. This study develops new statistical methods to detect when results are being deliberately manipulated versus when they simply appear clustered near thresholds due to normal rounding of measurements. We apply our new approach to lead testing data under the Safe Drinking Water Act and find evidence of manipulation in some monitoring results.

Biofuels: Past, Present, and Future

Authors: Gabriel E. Lade and Aaron Smith Year: 2025 Publication: Annual Review of Resource Economics, Vol. 17 Link:

Summary: The Renewable Fuel Standard, passed two decades ago, made biofuels a permanent part of America’s fuel supply, but the policy has faced ongoing challenges. Early concerns focused on whether biofuels drove up food prices and converted farmland, while failing to make advanced cellulosic ethanol economically viable. Today, the biofuel landscape has shifted toward renewable diesel and state-level policies, with new interest in sustainable aviation fuel, though the old worries about food prices, land use, and the cost of truly low-carbon fuels have resurfaced.

Size-based Regulation and Water Quality: Evidence from the Iowa Hog Industry

Authors: Chen-Ti Chen, Gabriel E. Lade, David Keiser, and John Crespi Year: 2025 Publication: American Journal of Agricultural Economics Link:

Summary: Large-scale hog farms have raised concerns about water pollution, prompting regulations in 2003 that targeted the biggest operations. While these rules successfully reduced ammonia pollution from large Iowa hog farms by 6-8%, pollution increased downstream of mid-sized hog farms that weren’t covered by the regulations. The overall impact on water quality in Iowa was probably minimal since mid-sized operations have grown faster than large ones.
